What is Calisthenics?

Calisthenics is a form of exercise that relies on your own body weight for resistance rather than machines or heavy equipment. It focuses on natural, functional movements such as push-ups, pull-ups, squats, dips, and planks, while advanced practitioners also take on skills like handstands, muscle ups, and planches. The word “calisthenics” comes from the Greek words kallos meaning beauty and sthenos meaning strength, which together translate to “beautiful strength.”

The practice of calisthenics began in ancient Greece around 600–400 BCE. Greek soldiers and athletes, including the Spartans, used bodyweight training to build strength, endurance, and discipline. Later, during the Roman Empire, soldiers also relied on similar exercises to stay in peak condition for battle. By the 18th and 19th centuries in Europe, calisthenics was promoted as a way to improve both the body and the mind. It was introduced into schools, particularly in Germany, Sweden, and the United States, where it became a foundation of physical education. In the 20th century, calisthenics continued to spread through military training and gym classes worldwide. Today, calisthenics has evolved into what is widely known as street workout culture. Athletes and fitness enthusiasts perform routines not only to build strength and endurance but also to showcase creativity and skill in parks, gyms, and competitions across the globe.
